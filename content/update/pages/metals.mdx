# Commodities FAQs – Your Questions Answered

## What Are Commodities?

Commodities are raw materials that support global production, transportation and manufacturing. They include metals such as gold, silver, copper and nickel, energy products like oil and natural gas, and agricultural goods such as wheat, cotton, coffee, cocoa and livestock. These assets form the foundation of many industries and play a significant role in shaping economic activity worldwide.

---

## Why Do People Trade Commodities?

Commodity trading originally developed as a way for producers and consumers to manage price risk on the goods they depended on. Over time, the market has expanded to include individual and institutional traders who take positions based on expectations of future price movements. Traders may buy or sell commodities depending on whether they anticipate prices to rise or fall, using these markets to diversify their portfolios or respond to global economic events.

---

## What Affects Commodity Prices?

Commodity prices are influenced by a wide range of factors because of their close connection to global economic and geopolitical conditions.

**Key influences include:**

- **Supply and demand changes:** Poor harvests, supply shortages or production cuts can reduce availability and push prices higher.
- **Geopolitical developments:** Conflicts or trade disruptions can affect supply chains, especially for energy and metals.
- **Industry demand:** Growing demand in sectors like technology, construction or manufacturing can increase the need for certain raw materials.
- **Economic uncertainty:** During periods of market stress, investors often turn to assets such as gold, which is traditionally viewed as a store of value.

## What Is a Forward Contract?

A forward contract is a privately negotiated agreement between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a specific price on a future date. Because forwards are traded over the counter rather than on an exchange, they can be tailored to suit the needs of the participants. This includes flexibility in asset type, contract size, settlement terms and expiry dates.

Unlike standardized futures, forwards do not trade on a centralised exchange, allowing for greater customisation but also introducing counterparty risk, since settlement depends on the parties involved.

---

## What’s the Difference Between Forwards and Futures?

### Forwards
- Traded over the counter (OTC) directly between two parties.
- Fully customisable, with terms such as quantity, price and settlement date agreed privately.
- Not standardised and not cleared through an exchange.

### Futures
- Traded on organised exchanges like CME or Eurex.
- Standardised contract terms defined by the exchange.
- Supported by clearing houses, which help reduce counterparty risk.
